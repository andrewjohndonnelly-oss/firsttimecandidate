Purpose — Your Why
The foundation. What drives you? This is the thing that will sustain you through the hard moments and attract people to your cause.
The Cautionary Tale In 1979, Senator Ted Kennedy was asked simply why he wanted to be president. He had no clear answer. It didn't just hurt that moment — it unraveled his entire campaign. Your ‘why’ must be visceral, clear, and true. Everything else is built on top of it.
Question 1 of 10
In one sentence, why are you running? If it takes more than one sentence, keep working on it.
Question 2 of 10
When you imagine your first town hall, what problem are you there to solve?
Question 3 of 10
Have you shared your ‘why’ with someone who will be brutally honest with you — a spouse, a close friend, a mentor? What did they say?
Pillar 2 of 3
Opening — Your Opportunity
Is there a real lane for you? This section forces you to honestly assess the electoral landscape — not just whether you want to run, but whether there is a real path to winning.
Question 4 of 10
What is the specific seat you’re targeting, and why this one?
Question 5 of 10
Is this an open seat, a primary challenge, or a general election against an incumbent? Do you understand what that means for your path to victory?
Question 6 of 10
Who else is likely to run? What do you offer that they don’t?
Question 7 of 10
Why is now the right time — for you, and for your community?
Pillar 3 of 3
Resources — Your Means
Resources are not fixed — you build them. But you need an honest baseline before you can build anything.
Question 8 of 10
How much time can you realistically commit per week to this campaign?
Question 9 of 10
Think about your existing network — professional, personal, community. How many people would take your call today, and how many would donate if you asked?
Question 10 of 10
Have you had an honest conversation with your family about what this will cost them — in time, money, and privacy?
